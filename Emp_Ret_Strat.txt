#How many employees have left the company?

df['Attrition'].value_counts()

#Q2 What percentage of employees leave the company?

attrition_rate = df['Attrition'].value_counts(normalize=True)*100
attrition_rate

### Conclusion

The analysis shows that approximately **16% of employees leave the company**, while about **84% of employees stay in the organization**.

This indicates that although the majority of employees remain with the company, a noticeable percentage of employees leave the organization.

# Q3 Which department has the highest attrition?

dept_attrition_percent = pd.crosstab(df['Department'], df['Attrition'], normalize='index') * 100
dept_attrition_percent = dept_attrition_percent.round(2)
dept_attrition_percent

### Conclusion

The analysis shows that the **Sales department has the highest attrition rate at approximately 20.6%**, followed by the Human Resources department at around 19%.

The Research & Development department has the lowest attrition rate at approximately 13.8%.

This suggests that employee retention strategies may need more focus in the Sales department.

# Q4 Does salary affect attrition?

salary_attrition = df.groupby('Attrition')['MonthlyIncome'].mean().round(2)
salary_attrition

### Conclusion

The analysis shows that employees who leave the company tend to have **lower average monthly income** compared to employees who stay.

This suggests that salary may be an important factor influencing employee attrition, as employees with lower salaries may be more likely to seek better opportunities elsewhere.

# Q5 Does overtime increase attrition?

overtime_attrition = pd.crosstab(df['OverTime'],df['Attrition'])
overtime_attrition

### Conclusion

The analysis shows that employees who work overtime have a significantly higher attrition rate compared to those who do not work overtime.

Approximately 30% of employees who work overtime leave the company, while only about 10% of employees who do not work overtime leave.

This suggests that excessive workload or overtime may contribute to employee dissatisfaction and higher attrition.

# Q6 Do job satisfaction and work-life balance affect retention?

satisfaction_analysis = df.groupby('Attrition')[['JobSatisfaction','WorkLifeBalance']].mean().round(2)
satisfaction_analysis

### Conclusion

The analysis shows that employees who leave the company tend to have **lower job satisfaction and slightly poorer work-life balance** compared to employees who stay.

This suggests that improving job satisfaction and maintaining a healthy work-life balance may help reduce employee attrition and improve retention.

# Q7 Which age group leaves the most?

bins = [18,25,35,45,55,60]
labels = ['18-25','26-35','36-45','46-55','56+']
df['AgeGroup'] = pd.cut(df['Age'], bins=bins, labels=labels)

age_attrition = pd.crosstab(df['AgeGroup'], df['Attrition'])
age_attrition

### Conclusion

The analysis shows that the **26–35 age group has the highest number of employees leaving the company**, with 116 employees leaving the organization.

This suggests that employees in this age group are more likely to explore better career opportunities, higher salaries, or professional growth.

Therefore, organizations may need to focus more on retention strategies for employees in the 26–35 age group.

# Q8 Does distance from home influence attrition?

distance_attrition = df.groupby('Attrition')['DistanceFromHome'].mean().round(2)
distance_attrition

### Conclusion

The analysis shows that employees who leave the company have a higher average distance from home (10.63) compared to employees who stay in the company (8.92).

This suggests that longer commuting distance may influence employee attrition, as employees who live farther from the workplace may experience more travel-related challenges.

# Q9 Can we predict which employess may leave?

attrition_factors = df.groupby('Attrition')[['MonthlyIncome','JobSatisfaction','WorkLifeBalance','DistanceFromHome','Age']].mean().round(2)
attrition_factors

### Conclusion

Based on the analysis, several factors appear to influence employee attrition in the organization.

The key factors associated with higher attrition include:

- Employees working overtime
- Lower monthly income
- Lower job satisfaction
- Poor work-life balance
- Longer distance from home
- Employees in the 26–35 age group

These factors indicate that employee attrition is influenced by both work-related conditions and personal factors.

Using these variables, organizations can build predictive models such as Logistic Regression, Decision Trees, or Random Forest to identify employees who may be at risk of leaving the company.
